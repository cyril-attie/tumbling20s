What are crises? Problems or opportunities?

The crystal clear criteria of a Crisis happening is that prices of assets fall sharply, behind 50% of their peak price in a short period, less than ~3 years.

Though crises are associated to a problem for the economy, those who know what a crisis really is call it an opportunity.

Indeed, those that had savings invested in some asset become frightened of having lost 50% of their wealth. That happens to all those who were not informed on how to foresee the Crisis. That is why this website alerts people in advance so they can take decisions before the storm.

For most people the crisis becomes a bad time. Savers, having lost wealth, consume less. So businesses struggle to earn revenue. As people realizes how much money they have lost shopping sales slow down and store inventories pile up. Eventually companies lower prices in a desperate search for revenue. But wages cannot be lowered (not below the minimum legal threshold). Consequently companies fire employees as they fail to earn enough money to pay liabilities. The blossoming unemployed people difficults even more the rush for revenue, and falling revenues sweep a great portion of profits. In the stock market, a fair price for a company is 10 times the profits of the company. With a disappearing profits stock prices fall even more, below 50% their peak price. And that is, in short, a Crisis happening.

But there's a right side to everything. People that can dispose of money during the crisis can get very cheap prices. This group can be divided in two major categories:

  1. Those that have savings
  2. Those that can take a credit

The cheaper the price of an investment the lower the risk. To be prepared for a crisis means having savings or credit in order to buy at a low price and risk. On the selling side of the market panic, frustration and anxiety is taking over. People that invested at a high price want to see prices come back to their peak. But prices plunge spreading negativity. Unemployed people also thinks negatively. Crisis-induced negativity gave its name to economic depressions. An economic depression happens after a crisis, which is after prices have fallen. Entrepreneurs are negative about starting a new business, unemployed loose hope about finding better jobs, consumption falls, production piles up in shops and then production stays depressed for a long period.

But government personnel, not like entrepreneurs, do not need to worry about giving back credit and even have incentives to spend: gaining popularity among recipients of the spending and among bankers. Popularity is welcomed to be elected again. Bankers finance the government spending as well as the political campaigns. Actually all the banking system depends on government reflating money by taking a big credit, otherwise they would need to throw bills form an helicopter.

Money reflation is commonly called monetary stimulus. The government is the only entity that can relaunch the economy by taking a big credit and pouring this new money into the circulation. As soon as the government and the medias speak about spending, "stimulate" or "rescue" the economy, the mood reverts. The entrepreneur public starts believing the prices will eventually turn up again. Then entrepreneurs take credit to acquire capital at the cheap price. And the cycle loops.

So crises are also the opportunity for governments to make the expensive projects. Expensive government projects are wars or infrastructure: like roads, hospitals, public transport, schools and administration. In every country a fake dialectical opposition collaborates towards expanding debt: left vs right, red vs blue, progressists vs "conservatives", communists vs "capitalists" (quotes indicate words are misused). This opposition is always willing to spend in order to gain popularity albeit among different groups. The left-communist prefers to spend on social measures, favoring the low-income people and minorities. The right-"capitalists" prefer to spend on armament and wars, favoring military, industrial, technological and basic material companies.

From the economic point of vue both are suboptimal. Economically, the optimal government abstains from demanding products and services in the market, but rather serves the market orchestrating individual initiatives so they blend and gear together.

How crisis happen?

During periods of monetary inflation, the quantity of circulating money inflates due to credit. Mainly businessmen and speculators take credit:

  1. Businessmen buy capital products.
  2. Speculators buy assets to re-sell them at a higher price.
The greater quantity of money is felt as it becomes easier to find somebody willing to give you money what you sell, whether products, services or assets. Basically more money is bidding the same quantity of work and products available, so wages and product prices increase. In other words, the value of money falls. Product prices or wages increasing is called price inflation. Price inflation isn’t felt simultaneously by all people.

As credit granted to businessmen is used to buy capital products, the firsts to feel inflation are the traders of capital products. Typically military, industrial, technological and basic materials sectors. Their revenue abnormally increases as new customers, that wouldn’t buy without the existence of credit, come to buy products.

As capital companies increase their revenue. Accordingly they expand production employing more people and raising the salary of already existing employees. As the new money filters down to employees, all products sales increase and eventually product prices inflate. Consequently, sooner or later, most companies see their revenues and profits increase and attempt to expand production. To expand production, companies employ more people. Unemployment falls. As people find new job openings, wages on average increase. However, as prices increase too, a given same quantity of money is able to purchase less goods. In other words money loses value.

To avoid a too noticeable decrease in the value of money banks rise interest rates. Recall, profits equal revenues minus costs. Rising interest rates increases financial costs, so directly shrinks business profits. As projects become less profitable, entrepreneurs and speculators abandon their projects. They fire people, liquidate capital and give back their credit to the bank. Money flows back to the banks and piles up there. The circulating money deflates, gets scarcer. It becomes more difficult to find buyers at the current high prices. So all prices, products, wages and assets, tend to revert their uptrend and to start falling. The monetary deflation causes price deflation: all products prices fall. Then money is revaluating as a given quantity of money buys more products.

Expectations of lower products prices make asset prices fall even before revenues and profits decrease. A crisis starts before price deflation reaches product prices. Asset prices start falling towards under 50% of their peak price in less than 3 years. And this is how crisis happen. We could let prices adjust for natural demand. Capital would redistribute towards the most demanded industries, regardless of credit, and all production would be optimized to the customer demand. Instead a new monetary expansion is forced through credit relaunching the inflationary phase that will result in the next crisis.

Why crises keep recurring?

Prices crashing are opportunity times: entrepreneurs and speculators sell assets cheaper than elsewhen because of the urgency to pay off their debt. So prices stay at such a depressed level as long as no one takes a credit. In times of deflation, not any entrepreneur dares to take a credit. The bad times are too recent and the prospect of business are unexciting. Basically if nobody takes a credit again all the money disappears. If money disapears the trading would collapse and restart on another monetary basis, other than banking.

There is a detail, that is rarely understood, that I deem the most important fact of the monetary cycle loop. In order to be able to give back the debt, entrepreneurs and the government need rising prices. In a deflationnary environment it is increasingly difficult to raise money and so to give back the debt. That is why, on signs of deflation (typically rising interest rates), entrepreneurs give back their debt. To keep the prices steadily increasing, subsequent credits must be bigger than previous ones. In other words, the quantity of bank money is always increasing, which is against the very nature of money. Let me illustrate this with an example. You might not get everything the first time you read it, just keep reading till the end and it will get clearer.

Let’s imagine a society in which there are 100 ounces of gold existing. In this society gold is used as money. Every year 1 ounce is mined, and 1 ounce gets lost in the sea or nature. So the quantity of gold is constant. Some gold is circulating and some is stored. One of the people of the society makes a central bank and:

  • One day, referred as day 1 of year 1 everybody puts his ounces of gold in the bank in exchange for a bill
  • During the first 5 years, the bank issues 4 additional bills per year and lends them to entrepreneurs and speculators at 2.5% interest per year (Interest cost is a percentage of the previous end-of-year debt balance)
  • After five years, to dissimulate monetary (and price) inflation caused by the 2.5% interest credit granted, banks rise the interest rates to 6%. They keep the interest rates at this high level for 5 years from day 1 year 5 to day 1 year 11
  • The yearly credit granted increases by 4 every inflation period (so from year 11 to year 16, the quantity of bills issued through credit per year is 8 bills)
  • The yearly credit returned to the banks increases by 2 every deflation period (so from year 16 to year 21, the quantity of bills issued through credit per year is 6 bills)

So at the beginning of the first year there are 100 bills existing and circulating. And the bank stores 100 ounces of gold that back the bills.After 5 years the quantity of bills circulating inflates to 120 (initial 100 plus 4 per year during 5 years). The debt balance at the end of the fifth year is 21.55 bills (20 issued as credit plus 1.55 of accumulated interest costs of the 5 years). At this stage prices have been rising: the monetary inflation has caused price inflation.

Banks rise interest rates to 6% causing monetary deflation: entrepreneurs return bills to the bank to avoid financial costs. The circulating bills decrease instead of increasing. In other words, every year, 4 bills are subtracted from the circulating bills and put into the bank.

After 5 years returning money to the banks, the quantity of money has fallen back to 100 bills. But now prices are lower than at the beginning, do you guess why? Credits taken by entrepreneur in the first 5 years were used to buy capital products. These productive investments are giving results. Capital products have the ability to increase productivity, which is the quantity of products we produce per day (or any other unit of time). Capital assets acquired during the first 5 years are yielding greater availability of products. Greater availability of products result in lower prices. But remember, for governments and entrepreneurs to pay off debts we need stable or steadily increasing prices.

To compensate for the lower prices, the money to be poured into circulation through credit must be greater than the previous credit. Bankers increase the yearly credit granted by 4 every inflation period. So from year 11 to year 16, the quantity of bills issued through credit per year is 8 bills. Also, the yearly credit returned to the banks increases by 2 every deflation period. Therefore from year 16 to year 21 the yearly quantity of bills returned is 6 bills.

Due to interest rates the debt exponentially increases through time. And only an exponential production can cope with an exponential debt. But production is not exponential, and shouldn't be.

The only chance for people to pay the debt would be to devalue completely the bills. But it is absurd to devalue money, money was created to store value. To store the value of work between the times of production and of consumtion. The value of wealth, which originally was accumulated work. So instead, expansion and contractions of the quantity of money keep recurring causing crises.
