Blockchain Revolution Seed

Thirty years ago, already a small group of people was preparing the current worldwide revolution. Timothy C. May, one of the founders of the Cypherpunks email list, wrote in 1988 "The technology for this revolution--and it surely will be both a social and economic revolution--has existed in theory for the past decade. The methods are based upon public-key encryption, zero-knowledge interactive proof systems, and various software protocols for interaction, authentication, and verification" (appeared in The Crypto Anarchist Manifesto). Indeed, in the late 1970's some proficient mathematicians made new discoveries in cryptography that opened the door to new ways of interaction: public-key encryption and hashing algorithms allowed decentralized peer-to-peer systems (examples: 1978's paper A Method for Obtaining Digital Signatures and Public-key Cryptosystems by R.L. Rivest, Adi Shamir and Leonard M. Adleman).

However, at that time Internet was still slow and only used for military and academic purposes and personal computers were less than 10 years old. With the opening of Internet to the public in the 1994 personal computers doubled processing power and memory every 10 years. So the Cypherpunks and other fellow founders of blockchain had to wait till 2008 to actually succeed. Other blockchains had appeared before 2008: Hal Finney's RPOW (Reusable Proof Of Work) from 2004 was a blockchain akin to how bitcoin protocol works. That implementation didn't take off, blockchain founders learned some lessons from that failure.

The early blokchain community kept discussing and engineering more secure algorithms on internet forums. Nearly fourty years after the first presentation of public-key cryptography Bitcoin apppeared. Bitcoin is the first blockchain that suceeded. It was in 2008: in August bitcoin.org was registered and on October 31rst the White Paper appeared describing the project. All this was done anonynously and the code was uploaded to collaborative open source platforms, first to sourceforge.net and then to github.com, and developed 100% collaboratively.

Blockchains work based on 2 pieces of software: a node software and a client software. The node software is the bulk of the system code: nodes are the equivalent to a server. Nodes computers compute client requests and update their stored blockchain with new blocks having been found. Blockchain holds its name because the memory of the system is organized in blocks chronologically "chained" by a link to the previous block hash and a timestamp. On the other side client softwares are able to sign transactions using public cryptography.

The cryptography is the art of encoding or ciphering messages. Nowadays most applications use private cryptography. Under private cryptography the server and the client store a private key that both encodes and decodes the messages to be sent. Messages can be your username and password, photos, comments, videos, or any piece of data sent through the internet. Private keys are securely stored in your browser and in the server. However, as server contain the secret keys of all clients, they are a honey pot for crackers (crackers are criminal hackers). Once in a while servers get corrupted by some cracker and private keys get stolen allowing the criminal to supplant the identity of clients to send himself money from client accounts or whatever else he intends to do.

Instead Public-key cryptography is based on 2 keys: a public key and a private key. The public key ciphers data that only the private key can decipher. The public key is the equivalent to the bank account number and the private key is the equivalent to the account's owner signature: on blockchain, to send units of value, one deciphers units that were previously ciphered with his public key and ciphers them with the recipient's public key so that the recipient, and only the recipient, can decipher the and encrypt them with another's account public key.

Operation Economically Cryptographically
Send money, Buy something or pay the bill You authorize the transfer of units of the blockchain from your account to another's account Your client (phone, computer, credit card or any support for your private key) signs the transaction by decrypting previously received (refer to receive operation) units and encrypts these units with the destination account public key
Receive Your salary, a transfer, a revenue or any other incoming money arrives to your account Somebody else has decrypted his units and has encrypted them with your account public key
