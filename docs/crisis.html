Hello, Crisis!

The crystal clear criteria of a Crisis happening is that prices of assets fall sharply, behind 50% of their peak price in less than 3 years.

A crisis, it has been asserted, is a bad or wrong time for the economy. Not for those who know what a crisis really is. To test whether a crisis is bad, we would need to define an economic purpose. So that any action nearing us to our purpose is defined as good, and the rest is bad.

Let's observe for who a crisis is good and for who it is bad. The ones that have savings invested in some asset become frightened of having lost 50% of their savings. And that happens to all those who were not informed and did not know to foresee the coming crisis. For most of the people it becomes a bad time. Clients, having lost wealth, consume less so businesses struggle to earn revenue. Shopping sales slow down, store inventories pile up. Eventually companies lower prices in search for revenue. But wages often cannot be lowered (at least below the minimum legal threshold). Consequently companies fire employees because they fail at earning enough money to pay liabilities. The blossoming unemployed people difficults even more the rush for revenue, and falling revenues sweep a great portion of profits. In the stock market, a fair price for a company is 10 times the profits of the company. With a disappearing profits stock prices fall even more, below 50% their peak price.

But there's a right side to everything. People that can dispose of money during the crisis find cheap prices. This group can be divided in two major categories:

  1. Those that have savings
  2. Those that can take a credit

The cheaper the price of an investment the lower the risk. To catch the crisis cheap price one needs money. Having money means being prepared. Being prepared one gets to invest at a lower risk. On the other side of the market panic is taking over. People that invested at a high price take losses. Taking losses makes one become wary and mistrustful. Frustration and anxiety to see prices come back to their peak spreads. People that becomes unemployed also thinks negatively. Crisis induced negativity is an economic depression. An economic depression happens after prices have fallen. Entrepreneurs are negative about starting a new business. And I want to change this reaction. During periods of inflation, the quantity of circulating money inflates due to credit. Mainly businessmen and speculators take credit. Businessmen buy capital products. Speculators buy assets to re-sell them at a higher price. As the greater quantity of money is felt, its value falls. It becomes easier to find somebody willing to give you money for any work because there is more money in total. So happens price inflation: product prices go up. But inflation isn’t felt simultaneously by all people. As credit is granted to buy capital products, the firsts to feel inflation are the traders of capital products. Their revenue abnormally increases: new clients, that wouldn’t buy without the existence of credit, come to buy capital products.

As capital companies increase their revenue, they employ more people and raise the salary of already existing employees. Employees spend their money in a variety of products. So once new money filters down to employees,all products sales increase and eventually product prices inflate. Consequently most companies see their revenues and profits increase. To expand production, companies employ more people. Unemployment falls. Wages grow as people find new job openings. Money abounds in the hands of all. But prices increase, so the same quantity of money is able to purchase less goods.

To avoid a too noticeable decrease in the value of money banks rise interest rates. Recall, profits equal revenues minus costs. Rising interest rates increases financial costs, so directly shrinks business profits. As projects become less profitable, entrepreneurs and speculators abandon their projects. They fire people, liquidate capital and give back their credit to the bank. Money flows back to the banks and piles up there. The circulating money deflates, gets scarcer. It becomes more difficult to find buyers, people willing to pay for products or assets, at the current high prices. So all prices, wages, products and assets, tend to revert their trend and to start falling. The monetary deflation causes price deflation.

Price deflation spirals into a crisis. Asset prices fall behind 50% of their peak price in less than 3 years. Prices crashing represents an opportunity. Entrepreneurs and speculators desperate trying to sell their assets to give back their debt. So they sell assets cheaper than elsewhen because of the urgency. So prices stay at this depressed low level until massive new credit is granted. In times of deflation, not any entrepreneur dares to take a credit. The bad times are too recent and the prospect of business are unexciting. Basically if nobody takes a credit again all the money disappears. If money disapears the trading would collapse and restart on another monetary basis.

But the government, not like businessmen, does not worry about giving back its credit. Men in the government have incentives to spend: gaining popularity among receivers and among bankers. Bankers finance the government spending as well as the politician campaign. And all the banking system depends on government reflating money by taking a big credit. Money reflation is commonly called monetary stimulus. The government is the only entity that can relaunch the economy. Relaunch commerce by taking a big credit and pouring this new money into the circulation. As soon as the government and the medias speak about spending, "stimulate or rescue the economy”, the mood reverts. The entrepreneur public starts believing the prices will eventually turn up again. Then entrepreneurs take credit to acquire capital at the cheap price. And the cycle loops.

Crisis is the opportunity for governments to make the expensive projects. There is a detail, that is rarely understood, that I deem the most important fact of the monetary cycle loop. In order to be able to give back the debt, entrepreneurs and the government need rising prices. In a falling prices environment it is increasingly difficult to raise money. So it is to give back the debt. That is why, on signs of deflation (typically rising interest rates), entrepreneurs give back their debt. To keep the prices steadily increasing, subsequent credits must be bigger than previous ones. In other words, the quantity of bank money is always increasing, which is against the very nature of money. Let me illustrate this with an example.

Let’s imagine a society in which there are 100 ounces of gold existing. In this society gold is used as money. Every year 1 ounce is mined, and 1 ounce gets lost in the sea or nature. So the quantity of gold is constant. Some gold is circulating and some is stored. Imagine that on the first day of year 1 everybody puts his real money, the gold, in the bank in exchange for a bill. So at the beginning of the first year there are 100 bills existing and circulating. And the bank stores 100 ounces of gold that back the bills.

During the first 5 years, the bank issues 4 additional bills every year to grant credit to entrepreneurs and speculators. They grant credit at 2.5% interest rate per year, a fairly low interest rate. Interest cost is a percentage of the previous end-of-year debt balance. After 5 years the quantity of bills circulating inflates to 120 (initial 100 plus 4 per year during 5 years). The debt balance at the end of the fifth year is 21.55 bills (20 issued as credit plus 1.55 of accumulated interest costs of the 5 years). At this stage prices have been rising: the monetary inflation has caused price inflation.

To dissimulate monetary and price inflation banks rise the interest rates to 6%. They keep the interest rates at this high level for 5 years. This causes monetary deflation: entrepreneurs return bills to the bank instead of taking newly issued bills. The circulating bills decrease instead of increasing. In other words, every year 4 bills are subtracted from the circulating bills and put into the bank.

After 5 years returning money to the banks, the quantity of money has fallen back to 100 bills. But now prices are lower than at the beginning, do you guess why? Credits taken by entrepreneur in the first 5 years were used to buy capital products. These productive investments are giving results. Capital products have the ability to increase productivity, which is the quantity of products we produce per day (or any other unit of time). Capital assets acquired during the first 5 years are yielding greater availability of products. Greater availability of products result in lower prices. But remember, for governments and entrepreneurs to pay off debts we need steadily increasing prices.

To compensate for the lower prices, money to be poured into circulation through credit must be greater than the previous credit. Bankers increase the yearly credit granted by 4 every inflation period. So from year 11 to year 16, the quantity of bills issued through credit per year is 8 bills. Also, the yearly credit returned to the banks increases by 2 every deflation period. Therefore from year 16 to year 21 the yearly quantity of bills returned is 6. And so on.
