The Tumbling 2020's

Note: don't come from an economic background? Start here.

A premise to forecast is to have indicators that warn ahead of time of what will happen. I will build this forecast on the model exposed here that can be called the T20 model. I invite you to be patient. It hasn't been easy to create a model simple enough to be understood by everyone and, at the same time, helpful to understand economic happenings of our time.

We concluded monetary deflation and production surplus causes price deflation. As price deflation is the cause of falling revenue and profits, I have searched my indicators that warned of monetary deflation and production surplus. Rising interest rates and debt being paid off are premonitory of monetary deflation. Below you can see both measures. Interest rates since 1954 and total debt securities of all sectors since 1946.

The last two crises happened in spring 2000, named dot-com crisis, and in autumn 2008, named real estate crisis. At these times asset prices fell more than 50% in less than 3 years. Half of the wealth of people disapeared. Such a panic.

For the curious: unless explicitly specified, "interest rates" refers to the Effective Federal Funds interest rate: the rate set by central bank for banks to borrow funds from each other overnight (for just one day). This rate leads all other interest rates.

Important remark: you will notice that I always speak first about the US. The reason for that is that US dollars have been the world currency. Since 1945 massive inter-national transactions, like oil and debts, have been conducted in US dollars. Besides US was the biggest client and the biggest seller of the international market. So whatever happened in the monetary plan, inflation or deflation, in US was likely affecting all other countries; at least those who trade internationally.

Focusing on the dot-com crisis in 2000. Between January 1999 and spring 2000 oil prices went from 10$/barrel to 35$/barrel. Oil is used to transport products and make packaging materials, so an increase in the price of oil causes price inflation. Resulting in interest rates climbing from 4.6% to 6.5% in roughly the same period. From spring 2000 to summer 2002, in two years, the Nasdaq 100, a sotck market index of tech companies fell from 5000 to 800. Near 85% of savings invested in these companies disapeared. Other indexes displayed also great losses:

  • S&P500 (United States) from 1560 to 760 ~51% loss
  • Nikkei225 (Japan) from 21000 to 7500 ~65% loss
  • SSE (China) from 2250 to 1000 ~55% loss
  • HSI (Hong-Kong) 18300 to 8300 ~55% loss
  • DAX30 (Germany) from 8400 to 2250 ~75% loss
  • FTSE100 (United Kingdom) from 6950 to 3275 ~52% loss
  • CAC40 (France) from 7000 to 2425 ~65% loss
  • IBEX35 (Spain) from 13000 to 5250 ~60% loss
There are discrepancies between the indexes performance. However the common pattern is more than 50% loss in less than 3 years. The starting date was spring 2000. The trigger was a 2-year period of interest rates rising. And during the dot com crisis interest rates were lowered from 6.5% to 1%, that's a great stimulus. I could zoom deeper into the crisis. Bring out company profits statistics, unemployment and inflation figures to point out coincidences. However past is past and, luckily, better things to observe are happening in the present.

From autumn 2001 to autumn 2004 interest rates remained under 2%. The obejective was to make the public take debt again. The government heavily sponsored people wanting to purchase a home and financed wars in Afghanistan and Iraq took place. Quantities of US dollars were poured into the economy and consumerism started again. The monetary economy was reflated and in the fall of 2004 oil traded above 40$/barrel, which is a sing of inflation. The central bank rose rates for two years again. From 1% to 5.25% in less than 2 years. House prices, that had been rising thanks to the previous cheap credit, topped. And so did rates that stayed at 5.25% for more than a year. That was the indicator of a new crisis coming.

At the higher interest rate, people started defaulting on their mortgage. House prices fell due to demand drying out when supply was at its peak. Though the stock market and interest peaked in autumn 2007, prices didn't crash till summer 2008: the bankruptcy of great financial institutions in september 2008 panicked investors. In the 3 years following October 2008 the asset prices fell more than 50% because of monetary deflation:

  • Nikkei225 (Japan) from 18400 to 7000 ~61% loss
  • SSE (China) from 6100 to 1675 ~72% loss
  • HSI (Hong-Kong) 32000 to 11000 ~65% loss

Assets prices fell more than 50% in 3 years. In the attempt to reflate the monetary base, the central bank lowered interest rates from 5.25% in summer 2007 to 0.2% in Christmas 2008. Panic spread. The low interest rates alerted investors of a possible devaluation of bank money to worthless paper.That's why in the same period Gold broke its all time high of ~800$/oz. In the following 5 years gold skyrocketed to 1915$/oz.

Business was critically depressed. None wanted to take debt and the government debt to save financial institutions was not enough. The head of the central bank said he would throw money from an helicopter if necessary. Rather the central bank decided to buy assets, mainly financial assets like government bonds and stocks. This way they would inject money bypassing the need to find a debt taker. Bankers have injected like this $3,500 billion which is approximately 25% of the $14,000 billion existing over 3 years. A huge amount.

The below chart plots the central bank assets. Since 1945 until 2009 the central bank assets were stable, below 900 million dollars. Since 2009 the central bank has acquired $3.5 trillion. Though originally the assets of the central bank were gold and silver and their liabilities were central banks bills the game has changed. Since 2009 central banks store government and corporate bonds as good as gold. But these debts will be redeemed in the very same currency that their liability is. So basically when they print money to inject it, prices rise due to monetary inflation. When they retrieve money by selling the bonds in the market, prices will fall due to monetary deflation. That is a really assymetric game. Since 2009 they decide the turning points of the market as they are the biggest player in the market. They have infinite money, as they write the bank accounts. To buy something they just need to increase your bank account in some units and that's it.

Important note: the US central bank is privately owned by 12 regional banks privately, in turn owned by all banks beloging to the system. Ownership of the central bank is a must to contribute belong to the system. Stock of these central banks cannot be traded or pledged and, by law, earn 6% dividend on capital invested. This dividend shall be cumulative. Which means their share in the central bank can grow exponentially and then, the plan would be, to cash out future dividend when the equity share has grown. Compounding $1 at 6% for 100 years yields $339 for every dollars invested initially. Resulting in a dividend of ~$20 per $1 invested. That is for the US central bank that was the first modern central bank. In every country regulations regarding central bank profits distribution are different.

Furthermore, on the above chart, you can see the US central bank assets topped in January 2015 at ~$4.5 trillion. Since then it has been draining out $640 billion from circulation, roughly the GDP of Argentina. These two measures are strongly deflationary.

Here is a first bold statement (at least as of June 5th 2019): the stock market reached its highest high one month ago, for the next 3 years the price will fall below 50% of its current price. How do I know a Crisis is coming? Well among many other roles in life I have been a trader in the stock market. Trading the stock market lets you observe greed and fear are the unconscious forces driving market prices. Fear is shown when the prices are underestimated. Typically towards the end of a crisis when prices crash most traders are packining. Greed is shown when stock market prices are too high. High prices indicate future overexpectation. And that is what's happening.

Let me highlight 2 indicators of greedy overconfidence. First prices today are twice the standard. Historically, a company investment has always yielded ~10% while a debt investment ~5%. In other words, according to standards, a property investment is recovered in ~10 years and a lending investment in ~20 years. Nobody set these standards, they were naturally set, through centuries, by people preferences and willingness to invest. Centralized lending (central reserve finance) has made some companies grow exponentially, specially banks, industrials, basic materials and technology. The latter sectors are heavily involved in the finance-capital supersector, that is largely hipertrophied.

Second, a very subtle greed indicator. In times of greed investors want return, turning a blind eye on risk. This results in a market behaviour called inverted yield curve. In a normal yield curve, borrowing to long-term is more expensive yearly than borrowing to short-term and renovating the debt. This is 'normal', statistically default risk increases the longer the duration of the loan. However when stock market prices are overvalued, when a crisis is imminent, smart investors (usually high wealth investors) get out of the stock market. Where do they put the money?

Before crises, smart money moves to government debt investment. In the context of a crisis, the central bank will lower interest rates to start reflating asset markets. Smart investors don't know how many years will the crisis and subsequent depression last, so they cautiously take long term bonds rather than short term ones. Short term bonds would have to be renovated soon at the lowered interest rates, earning less. But by buying long term bonds smart money gets to receive a fixed income return for many years. A temporary refuge from the markets. Besides the higher interest bonds rise in price so that the yield (interest rate/price) equals the one of new lower-interest bonds. So smart investors gain double: a safe stream of interest payments and bond price revaluation.

Before summarizing the monetary and market indicators of a crisis, I would like to comment on one more. To pay off debt, we need to increase production faster than what debt is growing. Production can be increased either by increasing productivity per person, or by increasing the number of persons. In the last 10 centuries banking worked well because both were happening at the same time. But in many occidental countries we have reached a demographic plateau, or even a depression. If you look at the demographic pyramids below, you will notice the world population is growing steadily. But growth is not equally distributed. Population has been shrinking in developed countries like US, Germany and Spain. In countries like India population is stabilizing or steadily growing. Middle East and North Africa countries are experiencing the greatest growth rates.

Demographic redistribution is not without consequences. Within developed countries population is aging. The people who retires soon will need their savings. However too many elder drawing out their life time savings will cause a major crisis, a calamity.

To sum up ideas. Is the stock market crashing?

In the monetary plan interest rates have been rising and the central bank has started selling its assets. Since December 2015 the bank interest rate has gone from ~0.2% to ~2.4%. Since January 2015 the US central bank has sold $640 million of assets, draining out of circulation an equal amount. Monetary deflation sooner or later causes money scarcity, prices deflation and tumbling profits. As soon as the market anticipates falling profits a crisis starts: assets start tumbling.

In the market plan additional cash due to corporate tax rate reduction has boosted overconfidence and greed in the stock markets. Prices are twice the standard, so a downward correction is due. Falling profits can extend the tumble well below -50% giving place to a crisis. In addition, an inverted yield curve warns us investors are preferring longer-term bonds than shorter term, regardless of statiscal evidence of higher risk.

In the demographic plan, in occidental countries, a great portion of the population is arriving at retirement age. These people will be soon withdrawing their lifetime savings. A fall in the stock market could affect them. Fear of realizing their loss might make them procrastinate selling their positions, but sooner or later they will have to. The crisis could extend through the 2020's, turning into a general depression.
